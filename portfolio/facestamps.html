Design & Fabrication Inspiration

Ideation

Initially, real photos of the Radparvar family were desied for stamp making. To transition photos into stamp making images, they were brought into Photoshop and edited using the Threshold effect. Unfortunately, during this process, it was found very difficult to bring out the features desired through the use of Threshold and other Photoshop tools.

The best example from this process is seen on the right.

From there, the deicsion to use Bitmojis instead was made - as they allowed more decision and discretion about what features were desired and not desired.

Once an image type was chosen, stamp holder options were explored.

A simple backing was chosen, as opposed to one with a handle, so each character could be engraved on the back side of the stamps. This choice was made for aesthics and functionality.

Design

Step 1: Choosing Images

Due to the inability to take the proper photos necessary to create photo-realistic stamps, Bitmoji was leveraged to create each of my family member's face.

Each bitmoji was made from scratch to represent each respective person in my family, and then a facial expression was chosen based on their personality.

From left to right: Masoud (Dad), Zhila (Mom), Sara (Sister), & Rebecca (Me)

Step 2: Extracting & Editing Images

The raster Bitmoji images were traced, extracted, recreated, and redrawn to create the four unique stamp images. The initial images were created as negatives of the desired image print - represented by the white part here - to prepare for its use as a stamp.

While some visual components from the original Bitmojis were able to be transferred, other items, like eyes, glasses, and so on, had to be recreated from scratch.

Step 3: Image Outlines for DXF

Imagine outlines were created to cut the negative of the stamp. Once the negatives were cut, a positive version was made to cut the wooden stamp holder.

A test cut was done with acrylic to check the DXF file and ensure that all of the lines and objects were laid out properly for engraving and cutting. Once the cutting was complete, the four mock stamps were rinsed to remove the extra engraving material that was left on and they were examined before moving on to the rubber.

Wooden Holders

Once the acrylic tests were completed, rubber cuts began. The first set of rubber engraving wasn't deep enough, so a second was done. Once those pieces were removed, they were examined. Small adjustments were made to the cutting and engraving speed and power, and they were cut once again for a more finished look.

After the second set of cuts, all of the pieces were washed to remove excess material from engraving. Wooden holders were then crafted by editing the stamp files to make them positives. The wooden holders were increased 7.5% in size and then cut.

Finishing Touches & Assembly

The final pieces were then put into a water bath and agitated to remove the remaining engraving dust. Any additional remanence was removed by hand.

The final rubber halves were glued to their wooden counterpart to complete the stamps, as seen below.
