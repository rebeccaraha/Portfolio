Gifting Tree

Commercializing Intellectual Property - Carnegie Mellon University | Fall 2016
Team Members: Christian Baca, Jacob Thomas Davidson, and Evan Westphal

Scope

Develop a product or system which integrates the 'Connected Gifts' technology developed by Dr. Jon Cagan and Dr. Daragh Bryne.

Understanding the Technology

The 'Connected Gifts' technology enables two separated persons to give their loved ones a gift at any moment to brighten their loved one's day. The technology integrates a locked gift box with an app that a loved one can utilize to unlock the box with at any time.

The technology was developed in order to provide separated couples to give each other personal artifacts at any specific point in time they see fit - whether spontaneously or pre-planned.

Brainstorming

Equipped with the IP, a number of areas for product development were brainstormed. Toys were chosen as the area with the most potential for commercialization and therefore pursued.

Through initial brainstorming, many concepts were explored. The first concept that came to mind was a Pokemon toy, where the Pokeball included the 'Connected Gift's technology.

Connected Gifts + Pokemon

The Connected Gifts + Pokemon idea was explored as it seemed like the perfect marriage. The idea was explored and a model was developed where users could buy a Pokeball case which would come with pre-loaded tokens inside. Users could then play a video game and unlock the physical tokens that would unleash content once again within the physical game. This feedback loop was seen promising in the 'phygital' (physical + digital) toy space but wasn't seen as particularly compelling.

The token being used in the digital environment didn't seem to add a value in the layer of interaction it provided for the user that justified the cost and therefore the team felt that there was a better opportunity that could be tapped into.

Comparative Analysis

From here, a comparative analysis was conducted to better understand items on the market and how the 'Connected Gifts' may integrate into each.

In order to truly understand their capabilities and how the technology may apply, Hasbro's animinatronic unicorn, Star Lily, was acquired and user tested.

Animatronic toys were seen to be a large opportunity area. For this reasons, Star Lily was used as a working example of an animantronic toy which could be integrated with the 'Connected Gift's technology.

Star Lily was found to have two major modes of play, physical and digital, both which interact with each other.

These features, seen below, were explored to understand where players of game play could be added through 'phygital' play. The ability to use tokens in the physical environment to interact with Star Lily was seen as a more positive facet to an integrated system than in the Pokemon concept - where the token would have interacted in the digital environment.

Physical Features
  • Four Major Motions (Wings, Paw, Head/Neck, Eyes)
  • Sugarberry for Feeding
  • Horn Interaction
  • Typical Plush Play (Petting, Grooming, etc.)
  • Music and Audio Feedback
Digital Features
Three Play Modes
  • Sugarberry Orchard
  • Crystal Cavern
  • Rainbow Falls
Continuous Game Features
  • Wishing Well
  • Treasure Chest

Despite this breadth of physical and digital features, integration through an animatronic toy, such as Star Lily, was still not the correct fit.

User Research: Rewards and Gifts

Once again, the team went back to the drawing board to brainstorm. After many rounds of brainstorming, the concept of reward systems came into play. The technology seemed like a great opportunity to merge rewards with gamifacation.

In order to better understand this opportunity, five parents with children ages two to eleven were interviewed. These interviews revolved around their reward and gifting habits and how they interact with their children when separated - as opposed to any specific toy or opportunity.

From these interviews, the teams were better able to understand how parents interacted with their children and what opportunities would definitely not be a good fit.

Iteration One Design

Equipped with these insights, the team once again began to brainstorm concepts. By incorporating these insights, the team was able to develop a new concept, Ellie the Elephant.

Ellie the Elephant was designed to be a plush toy with a plastic, digitally connected, treasure chest attached her back. Parents could load Ellie's chest with a small note, a small gift, some candy, or anything they'd like to gift as a reward or while away and unlock it at any time they saw fit - enabling the child to access the gift.

This toy provided parents with the opportunity to interact with their children on a deeper level when physically separate and add suspense to the gifting process. Additionally, it was closer to the original vision of the use of the technology - which was very important for both inventors.

The team took this early stage concept for review and to test with users in order to understand where its true opportunities were and where there were gaps in the experience.

For educational purposes, this was the concept presented at the Midterm Critique.

User Research: Ellie the Elephant

Once a first iteration design was completed, user interviews were conducted to understand if, and how, people would integrated Ellie the Elephant into their lives. In total, nine interviews were conducted with users and one interview was conducted with a former-Senior Vice President at a top Toy & Game Company.

These interviews provided the following three major insights:

  • Parents prefer for toys at higher pricepoints be able to be used with multiple children.
  • A toy like this has to be a "gotta-have-it".
  • There has to be a build-up to final gift reveal to increase excitement and suspense.

The Gifting Tree

Based on the major three insights, all facets of form factor were redesigned while keeping the core mission and function the same.

In order to redesign the form, the team spoke abstractly about ways to connect a plush and physical artifact. From here a tree trunk, or stump, was seen to the optimal form and the Gifting Tree was developed!

The Gifting Tree's trunk was chosen to be broken into three sections in order to give parents flexibility in gift loading and gift giving - as well as the ability to disperse the cost among multiple children, providing more value to the parent. The multiple compartments enable parents to:

load multiple presents at once,

gift multiple presents at once

or have one compartment per child.

Three animals were chosen for the final toy, a koala, a sloth, and a bear. These choices were consciously made as the toy had to be a 'gotta-have-it'. Based on research and trends, all three were seen to be 'gotta-have-it' animals.

Finally, the most important feature of the product was considered to be the 'three moments of excitement'. Modelled after the way cinematographers build excitement before any major action in a movie, the Gifting Tree provides three moments of excitement to build anticipation for the child - as can be seen in the gif below. First, the stump lights up with a pulsing colored light, specific to the child's animal, to indicate that a gift has been unlocked, then the child places their animal around the tree, and the finally, the gift is unlocked!
